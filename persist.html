durable-transfer experiment · adaptive dual n-back

The honest test of whether training can produce a lasting gain that transfers beyond the trained task — the only version of "fluid-intelligence increase" that means anything.

Read this first — it's the whole point. Adaptive n-back is the single most-studied candidate for Gf transfer, and the evidence is mixed and mostly negative: the famous positive result (Jaeggi 2008) largely failed to replicate, and meta-analyses find strong near-transfer (you get good at n-back) but little-to-no far-transfer to fluid intelligence. So this is not a machine that adds IQ. It is an experiment designed to detect a durable transfer effect if one exists for you — and, just as importantly, to stop a practice effect from masquerading as one. A real result requires: (1) gains on the transfer probe you never trained, and (2) survival on the retention test after a break. Either alone proves nothing.

1 · adaptive dual n-back — the training

Watch the square's position and the letter. Press A (or the button) when the position matches N steps back; L when the letter matches N back. N rises when you're accurate, falls when you're not — so you always train at your edge (this adaptivity is what separates training from mere practice).

N = 2 trial 0/0 —

2 · transfer probe — a DIFFERENT task you never train

Rule-induction: infer the pattern, pick the next item. Parallel random forms each time (no item memorization). This is a proxy for fluid reasoning, not a validated IQ test — but because you never train it, a rising score here is the only thing that would suggest real transfer.

item 0/0 baseline

3 · retention / spacing — what makes it "durable"

Durable gains need distributed practice (short daily sessions, sleep between them — cramming doesn't consolidate) and a delayed re-test. Do ~1 training block/day. After ≥10 sessions, take a break of several days, then run the transfer probe tagged retention. Persistence = the post-training gain still there after the break.
